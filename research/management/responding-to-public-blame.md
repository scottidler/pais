# Responding to Public Blame at Work

Dan O'Connor teaches communication techniques for handling public accusations and maintaining power in professional confrontational situations.

## The Problem

When publicly blamed/attacked in a meeting, your brain freezes. Most people react incorrectly:

- **Defending** → drops your status
- **Explaining** → makes you look guilty
- **Reacting** → makes you look rattled
- **Talking fast** → makes you look scared

## The 3-Line Power Sequence

### Line 1 — Stop Their Momentum

> "Hang on. Exactly what are you saying I did?"

Deliver slowly, zero emotion, then pause. Forces attacker to repeat their accusation—most can't do it without revealing their real motive.

### Line 2 — Become the Evaluator

> "I want to be sure I'm understanding your concern correctly."

Shifts you from accused to evaluator. "People evaluate from above."

### Line 3 — Reframe to Strategy

> "So, tell me specifically, what outcome are you actually looking for?"

Breakdown:
- "Tell me" → signals leadership
- "Specifically" → forces clarity (passive-aggressives thrive in fog)
- "Outcome" → reframes from blame to problem-solving
- "Actually" → demands truth, exposes performance

## Advanced: The Validity Anchor

> "You can [say whatever you want], but you can't [back up anything you're saying]. And the reason is [because it's not true]."

**Warning:** Only use when you can "hold the heat." Backfires hard if used incorrectly.

## The SPAR Method

How to make these responses automatic:

1. **S**tudy
2. **P**ractice
3. **A**utomate
4. **R**espond

Goal: your mouth knows what to say before your emotions wake up and sabotage you.
